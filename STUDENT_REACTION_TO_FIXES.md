# The 100 Fixes, Judged by the Student Who'd Actually Use Them

*Not the engineer, not the professor — the 19-year-old (or the busy adult) who opens this app for 10 minutes between other things. What would I actually feel? Honest reactions, grouped by how they'd land, with the fix numbers referenced. The important part is the middle: a bunch of "smart" fixes would quietly make me want to quit.*

---

## Yes — I'd feel these immediately (do these first)

- **Partial credit in Builder (#16).** Thank you. Getting a whole 8-word sentence marked wrong because I flipped two words made me feel stupid and I stopped using Builder. Show me the one word I missed and I'll actually learn it.
- **"Review my mistakes" (#34).** This is the thing I most want and don't have. Right now my mistakes just vanish. Let me see what I keep getting wrong — that's the whole reason I'm here.
- **Never dead-end / Skip on every mode (#21, #24, #50).** When Story or Dialogue just spins forever, I assume the app is broken and I close it. A Skip button or literally *anything* to keep going would keep me in the session.
- **Hear the corrected sentence after I'm wrong (#14b).** Reading the fix is fine; hearing it makes it stick. Small thing, big for me.
- **Resume where I left off (#7).** I hate re-navigating to the exact mode+concept every time. Just drop me back in.
- **A rule reminder I can peek at mid-exercise (#35).** When I blank on por vs para, I currently leave the app to Google it — and sometimes I don't come back. Let me peek without losing my place.
- **Make my progress visible (#9, #39).** "3 of 11 mastered" with rings — yes. Right now I have no idea if I'm getting better, and that's demotivating.

## Fine, but honestly I'll never notice (good engineering, invisible to me)

- **Concept-aware distractors (#17), rubric grading (#18/#19b), response validation (#44a/#50b), caching (#20a), XP debouncing (#36a).** I won't consciously notice any of these — but I *will* notice their absence (a frozen screen, a "wrong" that was actually right). So they're worth doing; just don't expect a "wow." They remove pain rather than add delight.
- **Font size / contrast / touch targets (#45, #47, #49).** I personally won't touch them — but the classmate who needs them *really* needs them, so keep them.

## Careful — these would annoy me

- **A placement test before I can start (#3, #4).** I came to *practice*, not to take an exam. Six questions gating my entry feels like a chore before the fun. If you must, make it skippable and frame it as "want a shortcut to your weak spots?" — never a wall.
- **An onboarding carousel (#1a).** I will skip it in 0.5 seconds like every other app. Put the "how it works" as a re-openable `?`, don't make me sit through slides.
- **Confidence ratings replacing "Know it" (#28b).** More decisions per card = fatigue. "Again / Good / Easy" is great for Anki nerds; for me, one tap ("got it / review") is enough. Don't make flashcards feel like paperwork.
- **Daily notifications (#38a).** One nudge, maybe. Nag me and I turn off notifications forever — and then you've lost the channel. Make it opt-in and gentle, or I'll resent it.
- **Comprehension quizzes inside Story/Dialogue (#20b, #30b).** The reason I like those modes is they *don't* feel like a test. Bolt a quiz onto them and you've killed the one place the app felt like fun instead of homework.

## These might make me quit — even though they're "correct"

*This is the real warning. Several fixes are pedagogically right and motivationally dangerous. A student doesn't optimize for long-term retention; they optimize for "do I feel good enough to come back tomorrow."*

- **Locking modes/concepts until I pass a threshold (#2b, #5).** Do not fence me out. If I want to try Dialogue on day one even though I "shouldn't," let me. Being told "you haven't earned this yet" is patronizing and it's the fastest way to make me close the app. Guide me toward the right order; don't lock the door.
- **Auto-making-it-harder / "desirable difficulty" (#10b, #16 framing).** I understand *you* know struggle helps me learn. But if the app keeps ratcheting difficulty right when I'm feeling good, it feels like it's fighting me. Let me *see* and *control* the difficulty (a visible toggle, #10a) rather than an invisible hand cranking it up. Autonomy keeps me here; a rigged difficulty curve makes me feel manipulated.
- **Demoting a "mastered" concept back to "review" (#39b — the forgetting-curve idea).** Losing a badge I earned feels like punishment, not science. I'll read it as "the app took my progress away." If you must decay mastery, do it softly ("time for a refresher") and never visibly downgrade a trophy.
- **Interleaving / "harder is better" messaging (#8b, #23b, #40b).** Interleaving genuinely works, but if the app *tells* me "this is harder on purpose and good for you," it feels preachy. Just mix the practice quietly and let me discover I'm improving. Don't lecture me about my own learning.
- **A "no error — it's correct" option in Spot-the-Error (#29a).** Pedagogically clever, but as a student it feels like a trick question, and trick questions make me feel dumb rather than challenged. Use sparingly, and never as a "gotcha."

## What the 100 fixes *missed* (the stuff I actually care about as a student)

- **Make it genuinely short.** My real constraint is time. A "5-minute session" mode with a clear finish line would fit my life better than any feature above. None of the 100 addresses session *length*.
- **Don't make me feel judged.** The single biggest factor in whether I come back is whether I feel dumb or capable. More than partial credit or badges, I want the *tone* to stay encouraging when I'm wrong.
- **Show me it's useful.** I want to feel this helps me *actually talk to people* — a "you could now say this in real life" line, or a real-conversation mode, would motivate me more than XP.
- **Streak insurance / forgiveness.** If I miss a day, don't reset my streak to zero — that "all my progress is gone" feeling makes me quit entirely. A freeze/grace day keeps me coming back.
- **Less reading.** A lot of the app (and a lot of these fixes) is text. As a language learner I'd love more audio-first, less wall-of-explanation — even the great explanations (#31) are a lot to read every single time.

## My honest priority order (as the student)

1. **Stop making me feel dumb** — partial credit (#16), encouraging tone, never a frozen "broken" screen (#50).
2. **Let me see my mistakes and my progress** (#34, #39) — that's the reason I'd stay.
3. **Never dead-end, always let me Skip** (#21, #50) — keep me *doing*.
4. **Give me control, don't lock or manipulate me** — visible difficulty (#10a), open access to all modes.
5. **Respect my time and my streak** — short sessions, streak forgiveness (missing from the 100).

**The pattern:** the fixes that reduce *friction and shame* would win me over; the fixes that impose *discipline* — placement gates, forced difficulty, mastery demotion, quizzes on the fun modes — are exactly the ones that, however correct, would make me quietly stop opening the app. Build the app around keeping me motivated enough to *let* the good pedagogy happen.
